# Chapter 11: The Global Evolution of Broad Listening

## The Deliberative Culture Behind Broad Listening

Broad listening first drew widespread attention in Japan during the 2024 Tokyo governor election. When Takahiro Anno used AI to make large volumes of public opinion visible, the term “broad listening” spread rapidly. However, the image many people took away at the time was what Chapter 1 described as “broad listening in the narrow sense”: a technology functioning as a “component” that uses AI to structure and visualize large numbers of opinions.

Overseas, however, the context has been different. Long before broad listening emerged, practices of deliberative democracy had already been accumulating. One important starting point was the *Planungszelle* (“planning cell”), devised in West Germany in the 1970s by Peter Dienel. In this method, around 25 randomly selected citizens spend several days discussing a specific policy issue and then compile their recommendations in the form of a citizens’ report. It became a pioneering model of citizen-participatory deliberation. Alongside these practical developments, in the 1980s and 1990s the philosopher Jürgen Habermas developed the theoretical framework of deliberative democracy, arguing that legitimacy in democratic decision-making arises from consensus built through dialogue.

Built on this accumulation of theory and practice, “mini-publics,” in which randomly selected citizens deliberate in person, were implemented in multiple countries. In Ireland, recommendations from the 99-member Citizens’ Assembly led directly to the constitutional amendment on abortion in 2018, while in France the 150-member Citizens’ Convention for Climate (2019-2020) formulated policy proposals.

From the 2010s onward, the emergence of digital tools such as Polis, Decidim, and Loomio drove efforts to expand the scale and frequency of deliberation. In Taiwan, vTaiwan incorporated Polis and became a pioneering example of continuously connecting citizen deliberation to policymaking. The 447-person citizen deliberation on regulating fraudulent advertising in Taiwan, discussed later in this chapter, is part of this broader trend.

In other words, overseas, deliberative practice came first, and broad listening was then introduced on top of it as a component for further improving and extending deliberation. The cycle of augmented deliberation explained in Chapter 3—“collection → structuring → deliberation → decision-making → explanation → renewed collection”—was accepted as a natural development of an existing deliberative culture.

On the other hand, it is not as though Japan had no experiments in deliberation. In 2005, the Tokyo Junior Chamber, using the *Planungszelle* model mentioned above, held the first citizen deliberation forum in Chiyoda City, and the practice later spread to other parts of the country. However, while there have been cases in which recommendations were reflected in individual measures, this did not develop into an institutional mechanism for incorporating the results of citizen deliberation forums into policymaking. As noted in Chapter 3, the *jigyō shiwake* process carried out under the Democratic Party of Japan government from 2009 to 2012 involved experts and politicians publicly debating the validity of budget items, and it led directly to policy decisions such as budget cuts and program terminations. Formally, it was one form of deliberation, but it was not citizen-participatory deliberation, and it ended with the change in government.

In short, Japan had spaces where citizens deliberated, such as citizen deliberation forums, and spaces where policy was decided, such as the budget-screening process, but they were operated separately. Citizen deliberation did not reach policy decision-making, and budget screening did not include citizens. Japan had little institutional experience of running a full cycle in which citizens deliberate, the results are incorporated into policy and implemented, and the outcomes return to citizens. As a result, when the concept of broad listening was introduced as a convenient AI tool for analyzing opinions, its original context—strengthening the cycle of deliberation—became harder to see.

This chapter therefore introduces examples of augmented deliberation from abroad. These are not merely case studies showing “how it is used overseas.” We will examine not only successful cases in which broad listening was incorporated into established traditions of deliberation, but also the challenges revealed by its application in conflict zones, where the basic preconditions for deliberation do not hold. By looking at both the conditions under which broad listening can be effective and the limits of what it can do, as seen in diverse international practices, this chapter aims to draw lessons for its use in Japan.
