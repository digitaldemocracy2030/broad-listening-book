## A. Core Concepts of Broad Listening

**Broad Listening**: A general term for technologies and practices that **use AI to structure and visualize many people's voices so they can be heard**, as the counterpart to broadcast, where "one person speaks to many." This book organizes broad listening into three forms: (1) as a tool, (2) as support for augmented deliberation, and (3) as politician- or government-led practice (Chapter 1).

**Plurality**: A democratic worldview systematized by Audrey Tang and Glen Weyl in their 2024 book of the same name. It supports the coexistence and cooperation of diverse positions through technology and institutions. The concept itself had developed around Tang, Weyl, and others before the book. This book positions broad listening as one implementation-facing aspect of Plurality.

**Augmented Deliberation**: Deliberation expanded in scale, frequency, and format through AI. It is an attempt to preserve the quality of small, face-to-face deliberation while extending it to thousands, hundreds of thousands, or even millions of people. Since around 2022, it has been discussed in parallel in both academic and implementation contexts, and related chapters appear in Audrey Tang and Glen Weyl's *Plurality* (2024).

**Issue Space** [book-specific]: A structured and visualized view of "what issues, axes of conflict, minority opinions, and blind spots exist" around a policy question. It shows a qualitative overall picture of "the perspectives through which people are thinking," rather than "what percentage support something" (Chapter 3).

**Public Voice**: In this book, the broad dictionary sense: **the total body of thoughts, opinions, and concerns held by members of society**. This is the sense used in the book title and in the Chapter 3 title. It is distinguished from "statistical public opinion" below (Chapter 2).

**Statistical Public Opinion** [book-specific]: Within public voice, the kind of opinion distribution measured by opinion polls through representative sampling, such as "XX% support, YY% oppose." This is a label used in this book to distinguish such distributions from public voice in general. The distinction is that broad listening is suited to public voice, or discovering voices, but not to statistical public opinion, or measuring representative distributions (Chapters 2, 3, 4.02, and 5).

**Representativeness**: The degree to which a sample reproduces the structure of the population in miniature. In opinion polling, it is secured through random sampling and a sufficient response rate. Because broad listening generally does not guarantee representativeness, this book positions it as a method for "discovery," not "measurement" (Chapter 2 and elsewhere).

**Valid Response Rate**: The number or proportion of responses returned in a usable form, relative to the number of questionnaires or invitations sent. This is a basic indicator for interpreting the representativeness and reliability of gathered responses (Chapter 10.02 and column).

**Open Question / Closed Question**: A question answered in free text (open) versus a question answered by choosing from predefined options (closed). Broad listening is characterized by its ability to handle large volumes of open-question responses (Chapter 2).

**Qualitative Analysis / Quantitative Analysis**: Qualitative analysis explores "what exists" in language; quantitative analysis measures "how much exists" in numbers. Broad listening is the former, while opinion polling is the latter (Chapter 2).

**Noisy Minority / Silent Majority**: The contrast between a loud minority and a majority that may have opinions but does not express them. Voices gathered through social media and public comments tend to skew toward the former (Chapter 2). The prototype is Richard Nixon's 1969 "Silent Majority" speech.

**Legitimacy Gap / Void** [book-specific]: In this book, a label for "the situation in which an issue that was not contested during an election suddenly emerges during a term, forcing politicians to decide without a mandate from voters." It is discussed as a problem that occurs more frequently as social change accelerates (Chapter 3).

**Uncommon Ground**: An expression Audrey Tang often uses. It refers to the shared basis that actually exists with someone who seemed to be in opposition, but that cannot be seen unless one looks for it. It is the philosophical name for the bridging opinions discovered by Polis through voting-pattern analysis (Chapter 11).

**Bridging Opinion**: An opinion supported by multiple opposing clusters. Polis surfaces these opinions and uses them as starting points for consensus-building.

**Point of Agreement**: A concrete statement with a high approval rate across different opinion groups. In Polis, these are extracted and shown as cards supported across groups.

**Civic Tech**: Activities in which citizens use technology to solve public problems. Representative examples include Code for America, Code for Japan, g0v, and DD2030.

**Digital Public Goods**: Open-source software, data, and standards that anyone can use and modify. Kouchou AI, Polis, and TTTC are examples. The United Nations defined the term in 2020 (column, "Public Goods and Digital Public Goods").

**Open Data**: Data published in machine-readable formats and under licenses that allow free reuse. In Japan, it is positioned under the 2016 Basic Act on the Advancement of Public and Private Sector Data Utilization and the government's Basic Guidelines for Open Data. This book discusses cases such as the Public Opinion Map 2024 voting data being released in CSV format (Chapter 4 and column).

**Cognitive Warfare**: A non-military attack that manipulates another country's public perception through disinformation or psychological influence operations. It is part of the threat perception behind broad listening practice in Taiwan.

**Fact-Checking**: The work of verifying factual claims in reporting or political communication against primary sources. Internationally, independent organizations such as IFCN define principles for it. This book discusses the merits and risks of a political party launching its own "Kokumin Fact" initiative (Chapters 4.02, 4.03, and 6.04).

**Sovereign AI**: The idea of completing AI operations using domestic infrastructure, data, and models so data location remains controllable. In addition to risks from dependence on overseas services and data leakage, it reflects concern that foreign AI models could mediate another country's cognitive warfare through training data or alignment-stage bias. It is discussed in public-administration and public-sector adoption (column, "Fujitsu's Initiative"; see also Cognitive Warfare).

**Selection Bias**: A phenomenon in which the mechanism for collecting data itself skews who participates. For example, party meetings gather only supporters' voices, while social media analysis skews toward high-frequency posters. It is related to the noisy-minority problem, but its cause is participation opportunity rather than opinion intensity (Chapters 2 and 3).

## B. Democracy and Participation Concepts

**Representative Democracy**: The basic form of democracy in which citizens elect representatives, and those representatives handle legislation, budgets, and administration. In Japan, House of Representatives members serve four-year terms and House of Councillors members serve six-year terms.

**Deliberative Democracy**: A democratic worldview in which citizens exchange reasons before majority voting and aim for agreement while changing their views when necessary. It derives from Habermas and is the broader category above this book's "augmented deliberation."

**Jürgen Habermas (born 1929)**: A German philosopher and sociologist. He systematized theories of communicative reason and the public sphere, becoming a theoretical source of deliberative democracy. In the 1980s and 1990s, he developed the framework that "consensus formation through dialogue produces the legitimacy of democratic decision-making" (beginning of Chapter 11).

**Agenda Setting**: The process of deciding "what gets placed on the table for discussion." Traditionally, survey designers and media organizations have monopolized it, but broad listening opens it to citizens by handling free-text responses.

**Arab Spring**: A chain of democratization movements that spread across the Middle East and North Africa from late 2010. Large self-organized demonstrations gathered through social media and led to regime changes in Tunisia and Egypt. This book refers to it as a typical case of a fluid movement without representatives (Chapter 11).

**Occupy Wall Street**: A protest movement against economic inequality that began in September 2011 in Zuccotti Park, New York. Thousands occupied the park under the slogan "We are the 99%," but the movement had no leaders or negotiating interface and ended after police removal without concrete policy outcomes. It is a limit case for connective action (Chapter 11).

**Connective Action**: A form of social movement presented by Bennett and Segerberg in their 2012 paper "The Logic of Connective Action," in which movements self-organize through personalized action frames on social media without formal organizations. Occupy Wall Street and the Indignados are the main examples in the original paper; this book also refers to the Arab Spring as a similar case (Chapter 11).

**Collective Action**: A movement form in which formal organizations such as labor unions and political parties mobilize resources and collective identity. It is contrasted with connective action because it has control capacity to "end" a movement through representatives.

**Echo Chamber**: A closed information space in which only similar opinions echo and outside dissent no longer reaches participants. It emerges from selective exposure and group polarization. The concept became widely known after Cass Sunstein discussed it in *Republic.com* (2001).

**Filter Bubble**: A state in which recommendation algorithms optimize for individual preferences and surround people only with similar information. Eli Pariser introduced the term in his 2011 book of the same name. While echo chamber emphasizes selective exposure, filter bubble focuses especially on algorithmic causes.

**Voting with One's Feet**: The phenomenon in which residents evaluate municipal administration by choosing where to live. It derives from Tiebout (1956). This book positions it as "another election" that makes broad listening necessary for local governments in an era of population decline (Chapters 3 and 8).

**Silver Democracy**: A critical term for the phenomenon in which policy tends to skew toward older voters' preferences because older people make up a high share of the electorate. It points to the structural problem that younger generations and children have difficulty getting their voices heard in politics (Chapter 5).

## C. Political and Administrative Institutions and Procedures

**Mini-Publics**: A general term for small meetings in which randomly selected citizens deliberate. It includes citizens' assemblies, citizens' juries, deliberative polling, consensus conferences, and similar forms.

**Planungszelle (Planning Cell)**: A method devised by Peter Dienel in West Germany in the 1970s. About 25 randomly selected citizens deliberate for several days and compile a "citizens' report." It is a source of Japan's citizens' deliberative meetings and Jibungotokaigi (beginning of Chapter 11).

**Deliberative Polling**: A method proposed by James Fishkin. Randomly selected citizens receive materials, deliberate, and are surveyed again to visualize "considered public opinion."

**Jibungotokaigi**: Random-selection resident meetings developed nationwide by the nonprofit Japan Initiative. They are a representative Japanese form of mini-publics (Chapter 8.01).

**Government Program Review (Jigyo Shiwake)**: A method used under the Democratic Party of Japan government from 2009 to 2012, in which experts and politicians publicly debated budget validity and decided reductions or program abolition. Formally, it is a kind of deliberation, but citizens did not participate, and it ended with the change in government (Chapters 3 and 11).

**Electoral Platform (Manifesto)**: A policy pledge document that a political party presents to voters during an election. In Japanese election contexts, the borrowed word "manifesto" is often used for this kind of concrete platform, especially when it includes numerical targets or implementation deadlines. This book generally uses "electoral platform," "party platform," or "policy platform" in English, and discusses platform development as an application area for broad listening (Chapter 6 and elsewhere).

**Administrative Procedure Act, Article 38 (Public Comment Procedure)**: The legal basis for Japan's public comment system. When an administrative agency establishes orders, ordinances, examination standards, disposition standards, or administrative guidance guidelines, it must publish the draft, solicit opinions broadly from the public, consider them, and publish the results (Chapter 3).

**Orders, etc.**: A defined term in Article 2, item 8 of the Administrative Procedure Act. It collectively refers to cabinet orders, ministerial ordinances, examination standards, disposition standards, and administrative guidance guidelines. It is a basic concept for determining the scope of public comment procedures (Chapter 3).

**Public Comment (PubCom)**: A system under Article 38 of the Administrative Procedure Act for soliciting opinions when establishing orders and similar rules. It has structural problems such as biased opinion counts and vulnerability to mass posting, and has drawn attention as an application area for broad listening (Chapter 3).

**Petitions and Requests**: Systems through which citizens express opinions or requests to national or local assemblies. Petitions are formal procedures based on Article 16 of the Constitution, the Petition Act, and Article 124 of the Local Autonomy Act, and after acceptance by an assembly they are reviewed through an introducing legislator. Requests have weaker legal grounding and are left to assembly practice. Organized voices are more likely to reach these channels, while the silent majority is less likely to be reflected (Chapters 3, 6.04, 11, and elsewhere).

**Participatory Budgeting**: A system in which citizens directly discuss and vote on how a local government's budget should be used. It originated in Porto Alegre, Brazil, in 1989.

**EBPM (Evidence-Based Policy Making)**: Policy making based on data and evidence rather than intuition or custom. Broad listening can become one source of judgment material.

**BPO (Business Process Outsourcing)**: A business model in which part of a company's operations are outsourced to a specialized external company, such as call centers or accounting operations. Altius Link, discussed in Chapter 9, is a BPO company for contact-center operations (Chapters 9 and 9.01).

## D. Tools and Projects

**Talk to the City (TTTC)**: An open-source tool released in 2023 by the US-based AI Objectives Institute. It classifies and summarizes free-text responses with LLMs and turns them into a scatterplot with UMAP. It was the starting point for broad listening practice in Japan.

**Kouchou AI (広聴AI, Broad Listening AI)**: Japanese-language OSS developed mainly by DD2030. It is based on TTTC, improved for use in Japan, and extended with functions for local governments and political parties. It is the central tool in this book (Chapters 10.00 and 12-13).

**Polis**: An opinion visualization tool developed in the United States in 2012 by Colin Megill and others. Participants vote agree/disagree on short statements, and AI extracts opinion clusters and "bridging opinions." It became globally known through Taiwan's vTaiwan process.

**Sensemaker**: An open-source opinion-analysis tool developed by Jigsaw, a technology unit under Alphabet (Google). Using Gemini, it automatically classifies thousands of opinions into major topics and subtopics and structures points of agreement and disagreement. It is designed to combine with Polis voting data, and appears in this book in cases such as Bowling Green, Ota City's Jibungotokaigi, and the Higashiosaka Roundtable (Chapters 8.01, 10.03, and 11.03).

**Remesh**: A real-time deliberation platform from the United States. Participants immediately vote for or against other participants' opinions, and AI extracts opinion groups. It was used in Israeli-Palestinian peace dialogue (Chapter 11.04).

**Idobata Policy**: An AI deliberation system jointly developed by DD2030 and Team Mirai. Behind the scenes, AI converts citizen dialogue into pull requests for improving an electoral platform on GitHub (Chapter 6).

**vTaiwan**: An online deliberation platform launched by the Taiwanese government and the g0v community in 2014-2015. Audrey Tang's participation in a g0v hackathon in December 2014 was a convergence point, and full operation began in 2015. It is a representative case using Polis for policy discussions such as Uber regulation (Chapter 11.01).

**JOIN**: Taiwan's government-run e-petition platform. When a proposal receives 5,000 endorsements, the administration is required to issue an official response.

**Baisoku Kaigi**: Resident-meeting support software provided by Plural Reality LLC. AI dynamically generates questions for each participant and immediately structures agreement, disagreement, and unresolved issues (Chapters 10.03 and 8.01).

**Decidim**: An open-source civic participation platform that originated in Barcelona, Spain. It provides integrated modules for proposals, discussion, voting, participatory budgeting, and more. In Japan, it has been adopted by municipalities such as Kakogawa City (Chapter 10.02 and elsewhere).

**AI Avatar**: An AI-created character that reproduces a person's face, voice, and manner of speaking. In this book, the representative example is Takahiro Anno's "AI Anno," which was used to answer voters' questions 24 hours a day (preface and Chapter 4.01).

## E. Organizations and Political Parties

**Digital Democracy 2030 (DD2030)**: A nonprofit open-source project launched in January 2025 by AI engineer Takahiro Anno and Ken Suzuki, chair of SmartNews and project professor at the University of Tokyo, and author of *The Smooth Society and Its Enemies*. Its main development projects include Kouchou AI, the deliberation platform Idobata, and the political-funding transparency tool Polimoney. This book is also being written by volunteers as part of DD2030's activities (Chapter 10.00).

**Code for Japan (CfJ)**: A nonprofit general incorporated association whose vision is "a society we think through and create together." Its main activity areas include local-government DX support, adoption of open-source civic participation tools such as Decidim, and civic-tech community building. It supports local-government use of Kouchou AI (Chapter 10.02).

**g0v**: A citizen-led open-source community in Taiwan that began in 2012 with volunteer engineers, designers, journalists, and others. Its idea is to replace the "o" in the government's official `gov.tw` domain with a zero, creating citizen-built alternatives to government sites on `g0v.tw`. Audrey Tang was once one participant in the community (Chapter 11.01).

**GovTech Tokyo**: A general incorporated foundation that supports Tokyo's digital transformation. In Shin Tokyo 2050, it built the infrastructure for data collection, integration, and visualization in-house and played the central role in broad listening analysis. It also promotes inter-municipal cooperation, including a January 2026 basic agreement with Hiroshima Prefecture and the Tokyo Metropolitan Government on collaboration in promoting AI utilization (preface, Chapter 5, and Chapter 8.02).

**Japan Initiative (Koso Nippon)**: An independent policy think tank established in 1997 as a general incorporated association. It is known for devising and implementing government program review, and since 2014 has developed random-selection resident participation meetings called Jibungotokaigi in municipalities nationwide (Chapters 8.01 and 10.03).

**Team Mirai**: A national political party founded by Takahiro Anno in May 2025. It won its first seat in the July 2025 House of Councillors election and incorporates broad listening into party operations and platform development (Chapter 6).

**Saisei no Michi**: A political organization that fielded candidates in the 2025 Tokyo Metropolitan Assembly election. This book introduces the case of Kazuya Obanayama, endorsed in the Musashino City district, who attempted low-budget broad listening in his campaign by combining Google Forms and generative AI (Chapter 7.03).

**CompDem (The Computational Democracy Project)**: A US nonprofit responsible for developing and operating Polis. This book also mentions it in the context of operating Bowling Green's large-scale dialogue (Chapters 11.02 and 11.03).

**Audrey Tang (唐鳳)**: Taiwan's former Digital Minister. Tang became a minister without portfolio in May 2016 and later served as minister after the establishment of the Ministry of Digital Affairs (MODA). Tang moved from participant in the g0v community to the government side and led Taiwan's digital-democracy implementations such as vTaiwan. Tang's co-authored book with Glen Weyl, *Plurality* (2024), provides a theoretical foundation for this book (Chapter 11 and elsewhere).

## F. Technical Terms (AI and Data Science)

**LLM (Large Language Model)**: AI trained on vast amounts of text that can generate, summarize, and classify natural language. GPT, Claude, and Gemini are representative examples. LLMs are central to Kouchou AI.

**GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer)**: A family of LLMs developed by OpenAI. It is strong at generation tasks based on the principle of predicting the probability of the next word.

**Word2Vec**: A method announced by Google in 2013. It vectorizes words from the surrounding context based on the distributional hypothesis that "words appearing in similar contexts have similar meanings" (Chapter 12.3.1).

**BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers)**: A bidirectional Transformer encoder announced by Google in 2018. It reads both preceding and following context at the same time and generates different vectors for the same word depending on context (Chapter 12.3.3).

**Sentence-BERT (S-BERT)**: A method announced in 2019. It improved the problem that plain BERT did not produce high-quality sentence vectors by learning sentence-pair similarity. Precomputed vectors enable fast large-scale comparison (Chapter 12.3.4).

**Embedding (Embedding Vector)**: A technology that converts words or sentences into numerical vectors with hundreds to thousands of dimensions so semantic closeness can be handled as distance. It is a foundation of Kouchou AI (Chapter 12.3).

**Cosine Similarity**: A metric that measures how aligned the "directions" of two vectors are, from -1 to 1. It is a standard way to measure closeness between embeddings (Chapter 12.3.2).

**Vector Search**: A search method that converts documents into embeddings and retrieves semantically close documents by cosine similarity. It can handle paraphrases that keyword matching misses (Chapter 12).

**Dimensionality Reduction**: Processing that compresses high-dimensional data into two or three dimensions so humans can view it more easily (Chapters 12.5.5 and 12.5.6).

**UMAP (Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection)**: A nonlinear dimensionality-reduction method based on manifold learning, announced in 2018. It tends to preserve global structure better than t-SNE and is used to generate Kouchou AI's scatterplots (Chapter 12.5.6).

**PCA (Principal Component Analysis)**: A classical linear dimensionality-reduction method that finds directions of maximum variance in data. It is the basic method behind Polis's public-opinion map generation (Chapter 12.5.5).

**Clustering**: Processing that automatically groups similar items together. Kouchou AI uses it to gather similar opinions.

**K-means**: A clustering method that repeats "place K center points, assign each point to its nearest center, move centers to centroids." It is simple, fast, and tends to equalize cluster sizes. Kouchou AI uses it for lower-level clusters (Chapter 12.5.3).

**Ward's Method (Hierarchical Clustering)**: A method that repeatedly merges the closest pairs and creates a tree diagram, or dendrogram. The user can later adjust how many groups to create by choosing where to cut the tree (Chapter 12.5.4).

**Spectral Clustering**: A method that represents connections among points as a graph and divides it at the most natural cut. It is strong with complex shapes, but easily produces "islands" (Chapter 13.2.4).

**Bridging-Based Ranking**: An algorithm that prioritizes opinions supported by both sides of opposing groups, rather than simple majority vote. It is used by X (formerly Twitter) Community Notes, Polis, Sensemaker, and others.

**Algorithm**: A clear procedure or calculation method for solving a problem. In this book, the term refers to different things depending on context, such as specific processing steps for clustering and dimensionality reduction, recommendation processing on social media, or Polis's bridging-opinion extraction (multiple chapters).

**Preprocessing**: The step of preparing data before analysis. It includes LLM-based opinion extraction, noise removal, spam removal, and structuring free-text responses. In Kouchou AI, preprocessing design greatly affects analysis quality (Chapters 4.01, 9.01, 13, and elsewhere).

**Prompt**: Instructions or input text given to an LLM. Output quality changes substantially depending on prompt wording, and the work of designing and iterating prompts is called prompt engineering (Chapters 12.4.3 and 13).

**Few-Shot Learning**: A method that lets an LLM solve a new task by showing a few concrete examples in the prompt. It removes the need to train a dedicated model (Chapter 12.4.2).

**Structured Output**: An API function that forces LLM output to strictly follow a JSON schema. It made it possible to incorporate LLMs as system components (Chapters 12.4.8 and 13.2.1).

**Hallucination**: A phenomenon in which an LLM generates plausible misinformation not grounded in fact. In Kouchou AI, source-text verification at the extraction step is one countermeasure (Chapters 12.4.5 and 13.2.2).

**RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)**: A mechanism that searches for documents related to a user's question, passes them to an LLM, and has the LLM answer. It makes it possible to handle internal documents and up-to-date information (Chapter 12.4.7).

**RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback)**: A tuning technique in which humans compare and evaluate pairs of answers, a reward model is learned, and the LLM is optimized through reinforcement learning. It was one of the core technologies that made ChatGPT usable for a broad public audience (Chapter 12.4.4).

**Reasoning Model**: An LLM that internally performs multiple steps of reasoning before answering, such as OpenAI's o1/o3 and Claude's extended thinking (Chapter 12.4.9).

**Token**: The smallest unit of text handled by an LLM. For Japanese, depending on the model and tokenizer, one token is roughly one to 1.5 characters. API fees and context limits are measured in tokens.

**Context Window**: The upper limit on the number of tokens an LLM can read and write at one time. At the time of this book's writing in 2026, major models differ widely, from hundreds of thousands to millions of tokens (Chapter 13.5).

**Long Context**: The capability of an LLM to read hundreds of thousands to more than one million tokens at once. It has the advantage of processing large volumes of opinions directly without embeddings, but it also has cost, latency, and "lost in the middle" issues, so hybrid approaches with RAG are also common in practice (Chapter 13.5).

**Hashtag**: A marker attached to social media posts in the form `#...`. Posts with the same tag can be retrieved together through search. This book discusses hashtags such as `#TOKYOAI`, `#シン東京2050`, and `#維新ブロリス` as entry points for opinion collection (Chapters 4, 5, 6, and elsewhere).

**Cloud (Cloud Service)**: A service model in which an organization uses large servers operated by another company over the internet instead of owning computers itself. In local-government adoption of Kouchou AI, "approval to use cloud services" becomes a major practical hurdle (Chapter 10 and elsewhere).

**Localization**: Adapting software or services created overseas to Japanese language and Japanese practices. In addition to translation, it includes UI, terminology, and operational adjustments (Chapters 4.04 and 9.01).

## G. Development and Operations Terms

**API (Application Programming Interface)**: An interface that lets external programs call software functions. The OpenAI API is one example.

**MCP (Model Context Protocol)**: A common protocol led by Anthropic for connecting AI models with external tools and data. Idobata Policy uses it so AI can operate GitHub (preface and Chapter 13).

**OSS (Open Source Software)**: Software whose source code is published and can be viewed, modified, and redistributed by anyone. Kouchou AI, TTTC, Polis, and Sensemaker all take this form.

**Fork**: The act of copying an existing OSS repository wholesale and developing it independently. Kouchou AI began as a fork of TTTC Scatter.

**Contributor**: A person who contributes code, documentation, issue reports, or similar work to an OSS project. In Kouchou AI, AI agents such as Devin are beginning to appear as contributors alongside humans (Chapter 10.00).

**Commit**: A unit in a version-control system such as Git that records source-code changes. One commit corresponds to one snapshot. Commit count is referenced as an indicator of development activity (Chapter 10.00 and elsewhere).

**Pull Request (PR)**: A GitHub mechanism for asking the upstream project to incorporate one's changes. In Idobata Policy, citizens' proposals are sent as PRs through AI (Chapter 6).

**PR Review**: The process in which other committers check a submitted PR and return requested changes or approval. It is essential to OSS quality, and a shortage of reviewers becomes a sign of project stagnation through merge delays (Chapter 10.00).

**Merge**: The operation of integrating changes from another branch or fork into the main line. In OSS, PRs are merged after passing review (Chapter 10.00).

**Facilitation**: The skill of encouraging discussion in meetings or dialogue settings, drawing out participants' voices, and guiding them toward consensus. AI facilitation, which supports issue presentation and neutrality, is spreading (Chapter 8).

**DX (Digital Transformation)**: An effort to fundamentally remake business processes, organizations, and service experiences through digital technology. In this book, it appears especially in the contexts of administrative DX and municipal DX as a tailwind for broad listening adoption (Chapters 5, 8, and elsewhere).
