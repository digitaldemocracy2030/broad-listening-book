## Public Comment Viewed as Broad Listening

When working on broad listening, we often hear the argument that “governments are already doing broad listening through public comment.” But when public comment is viewed as a form of broad listening, the public-comment system has many problems.

This column analyzes public-comment procedures conducted by the national government ministries and agencies. Public-comment processes run by local governments differ in their legal basis and subject matter, such as draft plans or draft ordinances, but they share the same structural problem: they ask people to comment on specialized documents.

### What Is Public Comment Actually Used For?

To begin with, where is the legal basis for public comment? It is found in Chapter VI of Japan’s Administrative Procedure Act. The opening provision, Article 38, reads as follows.

```
Administrative Procedure Act
Chapter VI: Public Comment Procedures, Etc.

Article 38: General Principles for Establishing Orders, Etc.
When an agency establishing orders, etc. intends to establish orders, etc., it must publicly announce in advance the draft of those orders, etc. and related materials, designate where opinions should be submitted and the period for submitting them, and broadly solicit opinions from the general public.
https://laws.e-gov.go.jp/law/405AC0000000088?utm_source=chatgpt.com#Mp-Ch_6
```

In broad terms, the law requires the following.

- When establishing “orders, etc.” such as cabinet orders, ministerial ordinances, review standards, disposition standards, and administrative guidance guidelines (Article 38)
- Publicly announce a draft in advance (Article 39, paragraphs 1 and 2)
- Solicit opinions from the general public (Article 39, paragraphs 1 and 3)
- Consider those opinions (Article 42)
- Publish the results (Articles 43 and 45)

In other words, public comment is used as a final check on rules that are already mostly complete. Most of the people submitting comments are companies directly affected by those rules, plus a very small number of unusually civic-minded individuals. As a result, most public-comment procedures receive fewer than ten comments.

The histogram below shows the number of comments received in public-comment procedures, collected from the public-comment website (https://public-comment.e-gov.go.jp/pcm/list).

![Figure: Histogram showing the number of opinions submitted to public-comment procedures, with most cases receiving fewer than ten comments.](../images/03_opinion_histogram_filtered_en.png)

Most public-comment procedures receive fewer than ten comments, so it is hard to say that they are “collecting views broadly.” One reason so few comments are submitted is that the process asks people to respond to materials written in administrative language. In practice, only corporate legal departments that routinely read administrative documents, or a small number of people who can decipher such materials, are able to submit comments. In addition, because these rules are at the stage just before implementation, they are written in highly practical terms, creating another problem: only practitioners can really understand them.

If you open actual public-comment materials on e-Gov, you can see that many of them are proposed legal amendments written with cross-references to article numbers and appended tables, such as “standards established pursuant to Article X, paragraph Y of the Enforcement Order of the XX Act and the provisions of Appended Table Z.” In most cases, you cannot even understand what is changing unless you have the original statute or notice open beside you and read it together with a before-and-after comparison table. To submit a comment, you need to understand both the legal position of the proposal and its practical effects. In reality, that functions as an entry barrier that limits commenters to industry groups, legal departments of large companies, and specialists in the relevant field.

As a result, for the government side, the public-comment system has become a way to create the appearance that “we broadly listened to public opinion in accordance with the Administrative Procedure Act.” For companies, it has become a way to “submit comments so that our interests are not harmed.” It is, in effect, a tacit collusion between “an administration that treats the process as proof that it listened broadly” and “companies that only need to protect their own interests.” Both sides know that the spirit of the law has been hollowed out, but because each side’s purpose is being served, neither has much incentive to change the system. That is why public comment has remained largely unchanged for so long.

So what happens when we view this as broad listening?

### Comparing Public Comment and Broad Listening Through the Double Diamond

The difference between broad listening and public comment becomes clear when viewed through the double diamond model of design thinking. This framework was proposed by the UK Design Council in 2005 and is now widely referenced as a basic model in UI/UX design and public-service design.

![Figure: The double diamond model of design thinking.](../images/03_double_diamond.png)

In the double diamond model, a divergent phase that broadens thinking and a convergent phase that narrows focus are repeated twice. In the first diamond, “collect voices broadly” (Discover) leads to “narrow down the issues” (Define), identifying **the right problem**. In the second diamond, “expand the possible solutions” (Develop) leads to “narrow down the solution” (Deliver), identifying **the right solution**. That is the basic structure.

Viewed through this framework, broad listening and public comment occupy entirely different diamonds.

- **Broad listening** occupies the first diamond: problem discovery and problem definition. Systems such as AI interviews and Idobata gather opinions broadly on the divergent side, while TTTC, Kouchou AI, vTaiwan, Polis, Change.org, and similar tools help issues emerge and narrow down “which issues matter” and “what should be solved” on the convergent side.
- **Public comment** corresponds to the convergence stage of the second diamond, Deliver. It is a mechanism for soliciting proposed revisions to regulations that officials have already largely finalized and consider ready for release.

In other words, public comment and broad listening serve entirely different phases. They are not opposed to each other; they are complementary mechanisms that divide responsibility across different stages of the policymaking process.

|  | Public Comment | Broad Listening |
|:--|:--|:--|
| Breadth of information | Comments from a small number of specialists and stakeholders | Many citizens’ sense of the issues |
| Depth of information | Legal and practical comments on a published draft | Diverse perspectives rooted in lived experience |
| Phase of use | Final minor corrections to a rule drafted by the administration | Discovering problems, collecting issues, and narrowing them down |
| Agenda-setting | Decided in advance by the administration | Raised freely by respondents |
| Position in the double diamond | Final convergence phase (implementation stage) | First divergent and convergent phases (problem discovery and definition) |

Research and development in broad listening is now beginning to address the later half of the double diamond: how to reach the “solution exploration and implementation” phases. Large-scale deliberation support for consensus-building is one example. But there are fundamental limits to treating the current public-comment system itself as broad listening.

Public comment is, at its core, a mechanism for checking a draft prepared by the administration and securing procedural legitimacy. It was not designed to “draw up a broad range of social voices.”

For that reason, the priority tends to be “satisfying formal requirements” rather than making the content easy for a wide range of people to understand. The administration also unilaterally sets the agenda and organizes the issues, so what will be asked, and what will not be heard, has already been decided from the beginning.

To truly call something broad listening, citizens need to be able to participate at the agenda-setting stage and before a proposed rule has hardened. The current public-comment system has a strong “we will ask at the end, just in case” character, which makes it fundamentally different from broad listening.

### Aside: Mass-Mobilized Public Comment and How AI Could Help

Looking back at the public-comment histogram, some procedures receive hundreds of thousands of comments. The cases with the largest numbers of submitted comments are as follows.

| Subject | Number of Comments |
|---|---:|
| Draft ministerial ordinance revising part of the Enforcement Regulations of the Act on Special Measures Concerning the Handling of Environmental Pollution by Radioactive Materials, etc. | 207,850 |
| Draft Government Action Plan for Pandemic Influenza and New Infectious Diseases | 194,832 |
| Draft ministerial ordinance revising part of the Enforcement Regulations of the Immunization Act | 173,092 |
| Draft ministerial ordinance under the Act on Welfare and Management of Animals (relating to animal care and management standards) | 168,036 |
| Draft ministerial ordinance revising part of the Enforcement Regulations of the Water Supply Act | 127,945 |

Although 90 percent of public-comment procedures receive ten or fewer comments, a small number receive massive volumes. Let us look at the results for the procedure that received the most comments, concerning the “Draft Ministerial Ordinance Revising Part of the Enforcement Regulations of the Act on Special Measures Concerning the Handling of Environmental Pollution by Radioactive Materials Released by the Nuclear Power Station Accident Caused by the Tohoku District-off the Pacific Ocean Earthquake of March 11, 2011, etc.”

This public-comment procedure concerned a proposed ministerial ordinance amendment to allow “removed soil” generated by decontamination after the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear accident to be reused nationwide in public works as embankment or landfill material. This column does not take a position on the merits of the proposed ordinance; it only describes what happened.

https://public-comment.e-gov.go.jp/pcm/1040?CLASSNAME=PCM1040&id=195240105&Mode=1

```
Number of submitted comments: 207,850
When comments that were identical character-for-character, including punctuation, symbols, and line breaks, were counted as one comment, the number of comments was 8,277 (about 4%). The remaining 199,573 comments (about 96%) were identical character-for-character to those 8,277 comments.
https://public-comment.e-gov.go.jp/pcm/download?seqNo=0000289933
```

The submitted comments contained massive duplication. Once duplicates were removed from more than 200,000 comments, only about 8,000 remained. This was not unique to this case. Among the public-comment procedures with the largest submission counts, there is a tendency for massive numbers of duplicate comments to appear, suggesting some form of organized mobilization.

In fact, the background was that multiple nonprofit organizations issued statements opposing this proposal and called for people to submit public comments. Some nonprofit organizations even told people that “comments can be submitted as many times as you like” and that “copy-pasting is safe,” encouraging many similar submissions.

On social media such as Facebook and X, posts also spread at the grassroots level encouraging people to submit public comments. Many posts used hashtags like the following.

- #汚染土壌バラマキ絶対反対 (#AbsolutelyOpposeSpreadingContaminatedSoil)
- #みんなでパブコメをしよう (#Let’sAllSubmitPublicComments)
- #あなたのまちに放射能汚染土がやってくる (#RadioactiveContaminatedSoilIsComingToYourTown)
- #放射性物質の排出基準を80倍に緩めるな (#Don’tLoosenRadioactiveMaterialDischargeStandardsBy80Times)
- #放射能汚染土を全国にバラ撒くな (#Don’tSpreadRadioactiveContaminatedSoilNationwide)
- #汚染土壌バラマキ絶対反対 (#AbsolutelyOpposeSpreadingContaminatedSoil)

Emotionally charged language like this spreads strongly on social media, and as a result, massive numbers of comments were submitted.

At the same time, by design, the public-comment system does not treat the number of identical comments as something to be considered.

```
In the public-comment system, the “content” of submitted comments is considered, not their “quantity.” Even if many comments with identical content are submitted, that number is not something the system considers.
https://public-comment.e-gov.go.jp/contents/about-public-comment/
```

If the quantity of comments is not considered, why do nonprofit organizations try to send such large numbers? There are three likely reasons.

First, when a large number of comments are submitted, the issue is more likely to be covered by mass media and to raise public awareness. It can also put pressure on the administration by making the volume hard to ignore.

Second, officials are obligated to read every public comment and must manually consolidate similar comments. When massive numbers of comments are submitted, the administrative workload increases sharply. As a result, the quantity of comments itself can function as pressure on the administration.

Third, because the public-comment system explicitly states that it does not consider the “quantity” of submitted comments, if policy does not change despite a large number of comments, the outcome can easily be received as “a huge number of citizens’ comments were ignored.”

Given this background, mass-mobilized public-comment campaigns are likely to continue. And because identity verification is not required, automated bot submissions may also become widespread. The fact that the public-comment system is vulnerable to organized external influence is an important issue for future institutional design.

In this case, people manually copied and pasted comments, creating massive duplication. In the future, however, AI could be used to automatically generate and submit comments. That would make it possible for more sophisticated mass submissions to proliferate in ways that simple duplicate removal cannot handle.

DD2030 obtained the original text of some public-comment submissions through information disclosure requests and used natural-language processing techniques to detect mass submissions and identify duplicates. If others are going to use AI to submit massive numbers of comments, we need to use AI to respond. Hirokazu Nishio, one of this book’s coauthors, prototyped an algorithm and wrote an article titled “A Ray of Hope for Countermeasures Against Mass Public-Comment Submissions.”

### Dividing Roles Between Broad Listening and Public Comment

As we have seen, public comment has structural limits. But that does not mean public comment should be abolished.

Article 38 of the Administrative Procedure Act clearly states that the administration “must broadly solicit opinions from the general public.” Public comment is supposed to have the role of “listening broadly to the voices of the public.”

But what about actual operation? As we have seen in this column, 90 percent of public-comment procedures receive ten or fewer comments, and most of the comments that do arrive come from specialists and stakeholders. There is a large gap between the legal purpose of “soliciting opinions from the general public” and the reality.

As a result, public comment effectively functions as “a final check on rules that are already mostly complete”: a place where specialists and stakeholders point out problems from legal and practical perspectives. That function itself is important. But public comment does not fulfill its original role of “listening broadly.”

Broad listening can take responsibility for that “listening broadly” function. In double-diamond terms, broad listening can handle the first half, “problem discovery and problem definition,” while public comment performs the final check in the later “solution implementation” stage. This division of roles has become possible.

The ideal flow would look like this.

1. **Broad listening**: Collect citizens’ voices broadly and discover issues
2. **Policymaking**: Draft policy proposals based on the issues discovered
3. **Public comment**: Specialists and stakeholders perform the final check
4. **Broad listening**: Use AI to structure the comments gathered through public comment and consider how to respond

In this way, broad listening and public comment are not opposed to each other. They are complementary mechanisms serving different phases of the democratic process.
