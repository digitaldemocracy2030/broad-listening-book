# Column: Collective Action and Connective Action: Turning Anger Toward Problem-Solving

By @tokoroten

This column introduces the political-science concepts of **Collective Action (organizational action)** and **Connective Action (networked action)**, then presents **the author’s proposal about the role of digital democracy**. To state the conclusion first: **we need mechanisms that convert the anger generated by social-media pile-ons into negotiable proposals and connect them to official routes**. The author believes this is the role of broad listening. The four-quadrant model introduced in the latter half of this column, and the idea of “connecting fluid public opinion to official routes,” are the author’s own organization and proposal, not an established academic framework.

## Two Forms of Action

When a social-media pile-on happens, many people express anger and the issue spreads widely. But that anger rarely leads to concrete solutions. Why?

Political scientists W. Lance Bennett and Alexandra Segerberg offered one answer to this question in their 2012 paper “The Logic of Connective Action.”[^1] They classified political movements into two forms: **Collective Action (organizational action)** and **Connective Action (networked action)**. Because *collective* and *connective* sound similar, they are often confused. The differences are summarized below.

| Perspective | Organizational Action (Collective Action) | Networked Action (Connective Action) |
|---|---|---|
| Means of mobilization | Formal organizations, resources, leaders, education and socialization | Digital media, shared platforms, memes |
| Identity | Collective identity (“we”) | A collection of personal expressions (“I think this”) |
| Frame | Collective-action frame: ideological, demanding | Personalized-action frame: easy to personalize, inclusive |
| Role of digital media | Only a cost-reduction tool; the dynamics do not change | The organizing agent itself; the dynamics change |
| Position of organizations | Foregrounded and visible as the core of coalitions | Backgrounded, or existing organizations are deliberately avoided |
| Representative or negotiating counterpart | Exists, with the ability to control when a movement starts and ends | Absent, often deliberately so, making it hard to appoint a negotiating counterpart |
| Typical examples | Labor-union demonstrations, G20 Meltdown | Arab Spring, Indignados (15M), Occupy Wall Street |

This column uses Bennett and Segerberg only for the distinction between the two forms of action and the comparison axes shown in the table. The later discussion of bargaining power and institutional connection, and the four-quadrant model, are the author’s own analysis.

**Collective Action (organizational action)** is a movement led by formal organizations such as labor unions and political parties. People first belong to an organization, and the organization invests money and personnel to form a shared collective identity.

Consider a labor-union strike. If an individual worker strikes alone, that person loses income during the strike. In most cases, they cannot endure the economic pain and have no choice but to return to work without having their demands met. A labor union, however, supports striking members’ livelihoods through a strike fund built from union dues. This allows members to continue striking without bearing the full economic pain individually. And because a labor union has representatives, it can both “start” and “end” a strike. That control is what gives the organization bargaining power. A deal becomes possible: “If our demands for better treatment are met, we will end the strike.”

The IRA (Irish Republican Army) is an extreme example. The IRA was an armed organization that sought the unification of Northern Ireland, which is part of the United Kingdom, with the Republic of Ireland. In Northern Ireland, conflict intensified from the late 1960s between Protestant residents who wanted to remain in the UK, the majority group, and Catholic residents who wanted unification with Ireland, the minority group. This “Northern Ireland conflict” continued as a de facto civil war for about 30 years. Violence among the IRA, Protestant armed groups, and the British Army killed more than 3,500 people, including civilians.

What matters here is that this is not some distant historical episode. The conflict did not truly end until the 1998 Belfast Agreement, and the IRA did not formally declare an end to its armed campaign until 2005. In the United Kingdom, a highly developed Western European country, armed struggle continued until just 20 years ago. What makes the IRA interesting as an example of Collective Action is that, as an organization, it was able to declare a ceasefire and agree to disarm. Even violent activity can be “ended” if it is organized. That is why it can become bargaining power. This is the defining feature of Collective Action.

**Connective Action (networked action)** is the contrasting form of movement. People do not belong to an organization. They participate based on their own judgment after seeing and hearing things on social media. Its defining feature is self-organization through a loose network temporarily connected by shared sympathy.

The Arab Spring of the 2010s is a classic example. Protest movements spread rapidly through social media from Tunisia to Egypt, Libya, and Syria, bringing regime change in Egypt and Tunisia. But although the movements succeeded in getting started, they struggled to design negotiating counterparts, decision-making, and institutionalization, and they did not lead to stable transitions. The causes differed by country and included political structures, the role of the military, and sectarian conflict, but the absence of a counterpart who could negotiate on behalf of the movement is widely identified as one factor. Egypt experienced a military coup, while Libya and Syria descended into civil war. Movements with weak designs for ending and institutionalizing can end in fragmentation, exhaustion, radicalization, and many other outcomes.

Occupy Wall Street in New York in 2011 was similar. Thousands of people occupied Zuccotti Park to protest inequality and spread the message “We are the 99%” around the world. But the movement had no leaders and no channel for negotiating with government or financial institutions. It ultimately ended with police removal, and its direct policy outcomes were limited.

Loose networks that arise spontaneously on social media have no organizer and therefore no ability to control the action. People gathered by anger can make policymakers and the media recognize a problem, but that does not mean they can solve it. Having no control means having no bargaining power. They cannot make the deal: “If our demands are met, we will end the movement.”

## The Structural Limits of Connective Action

Connective Action has three essential dysfunctions.

First, **leaderlessness makes negotiation impossible**. If there is no leader, there is no one to negotiate on behalf of the movement. Even if a government or company wants to negotiate, it does not know who to talk to. Even if it accepts a demand, there is no guarantee that the movement will end. Under those conditions, the other side has little reason to come to the negotiating table.

Second, **the movement is easy to hijack**. An unorganized movement is an ideal vehicle for already organized forces. Hijacking takes two forms. One is hijacking by activists or influencers whose livelihoods depend on social problems. If their influence or revenue base depends on the continued existence of a problem, they may have an incentive to sustain anger rather than solve the problem. The other is hijacking by existing political organizations. When people angered by a social problem begin a demonstration, groups carrying left-wing slogans such as “antiwar,” “anti-nuclear,” or “oppose constitutional revision,” or right-wing slogans such as “expel foreigners,” “reclaim the territory,” or “promote constitutional revision,” often join and redirect the movement. Some such organizations even pursue a strategy of gaining legitimacy by being repressed by the government. For them, repression may be more useful than resolution.

Third, **platforms have incentives for outrage, not resolution**. Social-media algorithms are designed to maximize engagement: likes, reposts, and comments. Posts that inflame anger spread more easily, while calm posts that propose solutions are buried. For platforms, it is more convenient for outrage to continue than for the problem to be solved. Our smartphone screens therefore keep recommending outrage whenever we look.

As a result, Connective Action falls into a double dead end. As long as it remains online, institutions can ignore it as “a large number of voices with no negotiating counterpart.” If anger escalates into demonstrations or direct action, it provides a pretext for exclusion by being labeled a “mob,” while the substance of the issue is shelved. Either way, the movement exhausts itself without reaching institutional change. This is the structural problem of Connective Action.

## Organizing the Terrain of Action in Four Quadrants

From here, based on Bennett and Segerberg’s concepts, the author presents his own organization and proposal.

![Figure: Four-quadrant diagram showing Collective Action and Connective Action on one axis, and negotiable versus confrontational institutional connection on the other.](../images/11_05_collective_connective_action_en.png)

This diagram organizes the relationship between Collective Action and Connective Action. The vertical axis runs from “negotiable” (high institutional connection) to “confrontational” (low institutional connection), while the horizontal axis runs from Collective Action to Connective Action.

- **Upper left** (Collective Action + negotiable): This is where political parties, labor unions, and industry associations are located. They pursue their claims through formal political procedures such as parliamentary activity, collective bargaining, and petitions.

In Japan, the spring labor offensive, or *shunto*, is a typical example. Rengo, the Japanese Trade Union Confederation, sets a target wage-increase rate; its member unions negotiate wages with management; and if they cannot reach agreement, they enter a strike. This ability to “start” and “end” a strike is the source of bargaining power. A workers’ strike may appear confrontational, but it is an exercise of the right of collective action guaranteed by Article 28 of Japan’s Constitution, and is therefore positioned inside the institutional order. Because it is a legally recognized right, management has no choice but to come to the table.

Industry associations operate with the same structure. The Japan Medical Association sends members to the Central Social Insurance Medical Council, or Chuikyo, an advisory council of the Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare, and directly influences the setting of medical-service fees, which are revised every two years. Member physicians are also local voters, and their organized votes during elections are a source of influence over politicians. The Japan Agricultural Cooperatives, or JA, works similarly. During negotiations over the TPP, the Trans-Pacific Partnership, JA strongly opposed the removal or reduction of agricultural tariffs and exerted major influence over the negotiations through coordination with agriculture-aligned lawmakers.

- **Lower left** (Collective Action + confrontational): This quadrant contains organized Collective Action that uses confrontational means, including armed struggle. The IRA and al-Qaeda are examples.

In Japan, the Japanese Communist Party during the period of its 1951 Program fits here. It adopted a line of armed struggle and engaged in direct action such as organizing mountain-village guerrilla units and the Bloody May Day incident of 1952. Later, at the Sixth National Consultative Conference in 1955, it criticized that line and shifted to parliamentarism, moving from the lower left to the upper left.

Aum Shinrikyo is an example that moved in the opposite direction. After Shoko Asahara, whose real name was Chizuo Matsumoto, and other senior members ran for the House of Representatives in 1990 and all lost, the group abandoned the parliamentary route and accelerated its militarization. It produced sarin and explosives, leading to the Matsumoto sarin attack in 1994 and the Tokyo subway sarin attack in 1995. Whereas the Communist Party moved from the lower left to the upper left, Aum Shinrikyo tried and failed to enter the upper left through parliamentary participation, then fell into the lower left through armed struggle.

- **Lower right** (Connective Action + confrontational): This is where leaderless, spontaneous movements are located. They may begin with social-media outrage or hashtag campaigns and develop into demonstrations or riots, as in the Arab Spring or Occupy Wall Street.

As described above, movements in this quadrant can raise social awareness of problems, but they cannot bargain. Because there is no leader, the deal “if our demands are met, we will end the movement” cannot be made, making connection to institutional change difficult. #MeToo and #BlackLivesMatter strongly moved public opinion, but their connection to institutional change varied greatly by country and context. In Japan’s former Unification Church issue in 2022, large-scale social-media opinion formed, but existing political parties on the “left side” of the diagram had to intervene before the issue connected to concrete legislation.

Taiwan’s Sunflower Student Movement is an exceptional case that escaped this quadrant. Students gathered spontaneously through social media, occupied the Legislative Yuan, and figures such as Lin Fei-fan emerged as leaders, establishing a negotiating channel with the government. Occupying the legislature had an effect similar to a strike because national politics stopped. A negotiation became possible: “If our demands are accepted, we will end the occupation.” The movement shifted from the lower right to the upper right. Another key to its success was that g0v’s 22-day livestream secured transparency and prevented violent removal by the government. This is discussed in detail later in the column.

- **Upper right** (Connective Action + negotiable): This is the domain where unorganized, fluid public opinion is connected to institutions. The scattered voices of individuals that arise on social media do not reach policymakers as they are. Mechanisms in this quadrant **translate** gathered voices into forms institutions can handle, such as points of agreement, demand items, and bills.

What belongs here is **not a tool by itself, but a combination of tools and institutional design**. Polis, TTTC, and Kouchou AI (Broad Listening AI, 広聴AI) are tools that collect and visualize opinions; by themselves, they do not guarantee the upper-right quadrant. vTaiwan is located here because beyond the opinions gathered by Polis, it has designed channels for negotiation with the government and connection to legislation. In the Uber regulation case discussed later in this column, Polis structured the opinions of more than 4,000 people, found points of agreement supported by both the taxi industry and Uber users, and after face-to-face discussion among stakeholders, regulatory policy was decided. Taiwan’s JOIN platform, where 5,000 endorsements oblige the government to respond, is an example in which the connection from opinion collection to government response is built into the institution. The scam-advertising regulation law developed through mini-publics is not a permanent institutionalized system, but it can still be positioned in the upper right as a case where citizen deliberation connected to legislation.

In short, tools provide the functions of “collecting voices” and “organizing voices,” while institutional design provides a channel for “receiving voices.” Only when both are present can the voices in the lower right reach the upper right. Even if Kouchou AI and broad listening initiatives by local governments and political parties aim for this space, visualized opinions will remain suspended in midair unless the receiving side is institutionally designed.

The constitutionally guaranteed “freedom of association” is a right that enables the shift from the lower right to the upper left: people with shared concerns can form an organization and gain bargaining power. But organizing takes time and cost. The author’s proposal is that **one role of digital democracy may be to make it possible to connect fluid public opinion to official routes without going through organization, in other words, to enable a shift from the lower right to the upper right**.

## The Structural Limits of Petition Tools as Seen in Change.org

Seen through this four-quadrant model, Change.org might appear able to occupy the “upper right.” It uses the official route of petitions to connect fluid public opinion to politics. According to its official site, it has more than 570 million users and is widely recognized as a “tool people want to use when a problem happens.”

But because of design problems, Change.org in practice remains a confrontational means based on overwhelming others through numbers, which places it in the lower right. Its promotion feature makes it possible to “buy” exposure for petitions, and the more money one spends, the easier it becomes to gather signatures. Under this structure, the number of signatures does not necessarily reflect the strength of public opinion. The democratic principle of one person, one vote is also not technically guaranteed; the design allows signatures from multiple accounts.

In addition, numbers such as “X million people have signed” easily become newsworthy, and media coverage brings still more signatures. News articles are then shared on social media as “evidence,” accelerating the pile-on further. Making signature counts visible can easily produce a positive feedback loop of outrage, and as a result, Change.org is often used as a tool of cancel culture.

In 2023, a petition campaign called for canceling the Japanese release of the film *Barbie*. The trigger was that the official US account had reacted positively to fan art featuring atomic-bomb imagery. But the petition campaign did not stop at demanding an apology; it developed into a demand to cancel the film itself. In 2024, a petition campaign was launched to cancel the release of the game *Assassin’s Creed Shadows*, drawing more than 100,000 signatures. The campaign began from dissatisfaction with the game’s historical accuracy, but instead of seeking a solution such as correcting the historical depiction, it developed into a demand to exclude the product itself.

What these cases share is that the petition activity aimed not at solving the problem, but at excluding the target. A tool that should have existed for problem-solving became a tool that deepens division. This is the problem with Change.org.

However, this problem is not unique to Change.org. It is common to petition tools in general when they are structured to “gather numbers and apply pressure.” The interaction between visible signature counts and media coverage, the payment structure for promotion, and the lack of technical assurance for one person, one vote are design characteristics that remain essentially the same even when the platform changes. Digital petitioning itself contains dynamics that pull it toward the lower right rather than the upper right.

## The Sunflower Student Movement: A Rare Case Where Connective Action Gained Bargaining Power

In March 2014, Taiwan saw the Sunflower Student Movement. Students opposed to a services trade agreement with China occupied the Legislative Yuan, Taiwan’s parliament, and continued their protest for 23 days.

Why did this movement succeed? Because although it began with people gathered spontaneously through social media, it ultimately gained “bargaining power” with the government. Occupying the legislature had the same effect as a strike because national politics stopped. And the movement had a way to end: if its demands were accepted, the occupation could be lifted.

### Leaders and Negotiating Channels

National Taiwan University student Lin Fei-fan and others became leaders of the movement and served as negotiating counterparts with the government. Ultimately, Legislative Yuan Speaker Wang Jin-pyng accepted the students’ demands. The forced passage of the services trade agreement was blocked, and in the local elections that November, the Kuomintang suffered a major defeat, causing a major shift in Taiwanese politics.

In other words, the movement was unusual because it “began as Connective Action, but then organized itself around leaders and behaved like Collective Action.”

### g0v and Audrey Tang: Transparency Prevented Violent Removal

The technical side of the movement was supported by Taiwan’s civic-tech community g0v, pronounced “gov zero.” g0v was founded in 2012 as a distributed community promoting government transparency and open-data use. Audrey Tang was an active member of g0v at the time. g0v’s basic stance was: “Citizens have gathered at the Legislative Yuan seeking conversation. People with different views and claims should first be made visible to one another.”

g0v provided several forms of technical support.

- **22 days of livestreaming**: From the first day of the demonstration to the last, g0v used YouTube Live and other tools to stream the inside and outside of the Legislative Yuan in real time. At a site where communications were almost destroyed, it secured smooth communication, and more than 48,000 people accessed the stream simultaneously at midnight on the first day.
- **Creating an archive**: The special site g0v.today archived video and transcripts so that anyone could later verify what had happened.
- **Information-visualization tools**: g0v developed data-visualization tools to help citizens understand the content and impact of the services trade agreement.

This thorough transparency was decisive. Once what was happening became visible to anyone, the government could no longer carry out a violent removal. Because the negotiation between leaders and the government was visible, the negotiating channel remained intact.

Audrey Tang later said that if you open your smartphone, you can become a broadcaster yourself, and you can also watch the stream and support the scene from afar. The lesson of “thoroughly opening information” from this movement later fed into the design philosophy of vTaiwan.

## vTaiwan and Polis: Consensus Formation Without Inflaming Division

The experience of the Sunflower Student Movement was institutionalized in vTaiwan, which began in 2015.

After the movement’s success, the Taiwanese government asked the g0v community to “create a large-scale opinion-gathering process.” g0v designed a multistage process combining design thinking and collective intelligence, and adopted Polis, developed in Seattle, as its core tool. Colin Megill, one of Polis’s developers, described Taiwan as an “incredible petri dish” for democratic practice.

vTaiwan is a platform where citizens discuss policy issues related to laws and regulations online and the results are reflected in government decision-making. It is operated jointly by the g0v community and the government. It has handled more than 30 policy issues so far, and more than 80 percent have led to some form of government action.

In parallel with vTaiwan, the Taiwanese government also launched the administrative platform Join in 2015. On Join, citizens can post policy proposals, and if a proposal receives 5,000 endorsements within 60 days, the relevant administrative agency is obliged to provide a written response within two months. Five thousand people is equivalent to about 0.02 percent of Taiwan’s population of around 23 million, making it a relatively low threshold for requiring a government response. While vTaiwan emphasizes consensus-building through deliberation, Join is designed to obligate government responses to petitions. The two are complementary pillars supporting Taiwan’s digital democracy.

### vTaiwan’s Four Steps

vTaiwan operates through the following four steps.

1. **Proposal**: Government agencies or citizens raise a policy issue.
2. **Opinion**: Polis is used for large-scale opinion collection and structuring.
3. **Reflection**: Based on the visualized opinion distribution, online or face-to-face deliberation takes place.
4. **Legislation**: Based on the results of deliberation, the government drafts and implements concrete policies or legislation.

### How Polis Works: A System for Discovering Points of Agreement

The core of this process is Polis, an opinion-collection and visualization tool. Polis was developed in 2012 by Colin Megill, Christopher Small, and Michael Bjorkegren. Megill, who studied international relations, witnessed Occupy Wall Street and the Arab Spring and began developing a real-time system for “collecting, analyzing, and understanding the opinions of large groups,” based on the problem awareness that “it is really hard for large numbers of people to communicate effectively.” Polis was open-sourced in 2016 and is now operated by the nonprofit The Computational Democracy Project, or CompDem.

Polis has a design philosophy fundamentally different from Change.org.

**Change.org’s design**: The goal is to maximize signature counts. The more supporters are gathered, the more successful the campaign appears. The structure rewards messages that inflame conflict.

**Polis’s design**: The goal is to visualize the “distribution” of opinions and the “points of agreement.”

Let us look at the concrete flow. Suppose Polis is used on the topic of regulating online abuse on social media.

1. **Posting opinions**: Participants post short opinions, such as “To protect freedom of expression, regulation should be kept to a minimum” or “To protect victims, platforms should be required to remove harmful content.”
2. **Voting**: Participants vote “agree,” “disagree,” or “pass” on every opinion posted by others.
3. **Grouping**: People with similar voting patterns are automatically grouped. Groups such as “freedom-of-expression priority” and “victim-protection priority” become visible.

The important point is that **all participants vote on all opinions**. Polis does not gather “supporters” as Change.org does. It gathers “diverse people’s reactions to diverse opinions.”

The innovation of Polis is that it analyzes this voting data in real time and gives special prominence to **opinions supported by both groups**. Audrey Tang calls this “uncommon ground.” Rather than “common ground,” the word “uncommon” emphasizes the discovery of an unexpected shared foundation: something one actually shares with the other side even though one assumed they were opposed. In the example above, both the freedom-of-expression group and the victim-protection group might support a statement such as “there should be some way to address malicious anonymous posts.” Discovering such “points of agreement people had not realized existed” is the essential strength of Polis.

Each time participants vote, they can see their own position in the overall opinion space in real time. On ordinary social media, algorithms make it easy to stay inside a “filter bubble” where mostly similar opinions are shown. In Polis, by contrast, groups with views different from one’s own are visually displayed. **People can recognize at a glance that diverse people with different opinions really do exist.**

### Uber Regulation: A Successful vTaiwan Case

The famous successful case for vTaiwan and Polis is the 2015 issue of Uber regulation.

At the time, there was fierce conflict in Taiwan over Uber’s legality. The taxi industry opposed it as illegal unlicensed taxi service, while Uber users argued that a convenient service should not be banned. It was a typical binary conflict.

vTaiwan conducted large-scale opinion collection using Polis. More than 4,000 people participated and hundreds of opinions were submitted. As a result, bridging opinions such as the following were discovered.

- “Driver identity verification and insurance should be required” (supported by more than 80 percent of both groups)
- “Deregulation of existing taxi rules should also be considered” (supported by more than 70 percent of both groups)

Based on these results, the government developed a compromise: rather than banning Uber completely or liberalizing it completely, it would legalize the service on conditions such as insurance enrollment and driver registration. Ultimately, in October 2016, Taiwan revised its Regulations for Automobile Transportation Operators and codified a framework for diversified taxis, taxi services booked through internet platforms. vTaiwan’s achievement was not that it made Uber “win” or “lose”; it **converted conflict into an update to institutional design**.

## TTTC in vTaiwan: Turning Free Text into Policy-Proposal Power

Since 2023, vTaiwan has used Talk to the City (TTTC) in addition to Polis. In the Recursive Public project, which received an OpenAI Democratic Inputs to AI grant, vTaiwan collaborated with Chatham House, a UK international-affairs think tank, and the AI Objectives Institute, which develops TTTC, to conduct citizen consultations on AI governance.

Polis and TTTC work in complementary ways. Polis collects preferences through voting and quantitatively shows the distribution of opinion groups and bridging opinions. TTTC, by contrast, uses LLMs to cluster free-text responses and workshop transcripts, structuring qualitative information about what people actually said.

### Converting Free Text into “Policy-Proposal Power”

Recall the problem of Connective Action discussed at the beginning of this column. Statements and pile-ons on social media do not have “policy-proposal power,” even when many people participate, because they are not structured.

Traditionally, the kind of data with policy-proposal power has mainly been yes/no voting data. Quantitative data such as “60 percent in favor, 40 percent opposed” is easy to use as a basis for policy decisions, while free text tends to be dismissed because it is “not quantifiable.”

What TTTC does is convert this kind of free text into “information with policy-proposal power.” Suppose 100 people express opinions in their own words at a workshop. As-is, those are just 100 individual opinions. But with TTTC, it becomes possible to visualize which themes those opinions fall into, how many opinions each cluster contains, and which opinions represent each cluster. This can be seen as a process for converting scattered social-media opinions into a “proposal document.” Noise becomes signal.

Audrey Tang has said that in 2014, even with the power of g0v, it would have been impossible to interview the participants in mini-publics and aggregate their opinions while preserving nuance. But now, with the help of Talk to the City, that cost has become almost zero. The emergence of LLMs has made it possible to structure free text at a scale that was once impossible.

## Broad Listening and Institutional Connection: The Author’s Proposal

Recall the four-quadrant model introduced at the beginning of this column. The author’s proposal was that one role of digital democracy may be to fill the empty space of “fluid + negotiable.”

The author believes broad listening can become a technology that enables this “institutional connection.” However, the depth of that connection has three levels.

1. **Broad listening as a tool**: Polis, TTTC, and Kouchou AI. These tools structure voices and produce information showing that “there are points of agreement,” but whether connection happens depends on the user.
2. **Broad listening led by politicians or administrators**: This approaches the upper right through the will of “the listener to respond,” but continuity is not guaranteed because it is not institutionalized.
3. **Augmented deliberation support**: Processes such as vTaiwan’s online opinion collection, offline stakeholder coordination, and policy decision-making, or JOIN’s response obligation. Because response is built into the institution, this stably realizes the upper right.

The author hopes that whenever a social-media pile-on occurs, tools such as Polis, TTTC, and Kouchou AI will be used. The energy of anger should be directed not toward amplifying conflict, but toward discovering points of agreement. Those points of agreement should then be connected to the policymaking process.

Change.org signatures apply pressure through “the power of numbers,” but that assumes conflict. Polis, TTTC, and Kouchou AI instead present structured information saying, “there is a point of agreement here.” For politicians, the former is a test of whether one is friend or enemy; the latter can be handled as a solvable problem.

Broad listening dramatically lowers the cost of responding to citizens’ voices. Until now, “politicians who could hear voices” were limited to politicians with a strong local base and large support organizations, or exceptional people with charismatic ability to read public opinion. Kouchou AI makes that response capacity available to anyone.

Connecting fluid public opinion to official policymaking processes without allowing Connective Action to be hijacked by particular organizations: this is one reason augmented-deliberation-style broad listening became necessary. Extracting points of agreement from thousands of free-text responses once required hundreds of hours of human labor. In the 2020s, LLMs dramatically lowered that cost, making broad listening that connects fluid public opinion to policy processes feasible. As a receptacle for the enormous energy generated by Connective Action in the social-media age, broad listening emerged almost inevitably when technological maturity and social demand converged.
