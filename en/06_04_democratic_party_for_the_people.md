# Chapter 6: Using Broad Listening in National Elections

Written by: tokoroten

## The Democratic Party for the People's Elections and Broad Listening: An Interview with Takae Ito

In March 2026, I interviewed Takae Ito, a House of Councillors lawmaker, at the House of Councillors office building, along with Mr. Yamada of Santo General Planning, the IT company responsible for supporting the design, implementation, and operation of the AI system. Based on that interview, this section describes how the Democratic Party for the People introduced and operated broad listening in the July 2025 House of Councillors election and the February 2026 House of Representatives election.

The party's broad listening initiative for the House of Councillors election proceeded in three stages: collecting opinions, analyzing them with AI, and reflecting the results in policy.

### Collecting Opinions: An Online-Offline "Melting Pot"

At first, the Democratic Party for the People considered purchasing all post data from X in order to capture even unconscious needs. However, as Ito explained, "When it came down to whether we could really pay 5 million yen a month for data collection, that was simply difficult for a small party like ours," so they had to abandon the idea.

Instead, they adopted a strategy of gathering data from every accessible channel: "eating all the data within a three-meter radius." Specifically, they collected voices from posts under the X hashtag `#国民民主党に伝えたい` (`#WhatIWantToTellTheDemocraticPartyForThePeople`), YouTube comments, Google Form survey results, phone calls through an AI-enabled call center, comments received at street speeches, opinions shared in roundtable discussions, petitions, written opinions, letters, and more. They gathered every possible voice, online and offline alike. Looking back, Ito said, "Rather than casting a net over the open sea to look for policy ideas, asking 'are there perspectives we are currently overlooking?' ended up bringing in many opinions that could actually be turned into policy."

There had been an earlier incident that drove home the importance of this kind of "gap check." Since 2019, Ito had worked on policy for menstruation and menopause. By 2023, menstrual symptoms had become accepted grounds for makeup exams at public high schools, and in 2024 a comprehensive women's health center had opened, among other concrete results. But because those results had already been achieved, the party platform did not give them special mention. That led to a wave of criticism: "Have you forgotten about us, when we are still only halfway there?" Space in a policy platform is finite, and it was natural from a policy-management standpoint to remove items that had been achieved. But for the people directly affected, it did not feel that way. This bitter experience became the starting point for the idea of using AI to collect and analyze policy needs again and identify omissions in the platform.

### AI Scoring: Matching 60,000 Opinions Against 300 Policies

For the July 2025 House of Councillors election, the party carried out a matching process between more than 60,000 collected opinions and its platform commitments, submitted bills, and related materials. Santo General Planning handled the technical side of data organization and AI processing for the matching platform. The AI was given more than 300 platform commitments since the party's founding, submitted bills, and even every question asked by its lawmakers in the Japanese Parliament. It then scored how well existing policy addressed each submitted opinion. The analysis was designed to improve how opinions were understood during the policy development process, and it also visualized how those opinions corresponded to existing policies.

![Figure: Part of a matrix in which AI matched 60,000 opinions against more than 300 platform commitments and assigned each pair a numerical match score. Each row corresponds to an opinion, and each column corresponds to a platform commitment.](images/06_04_scoring_matrix.png)
<p class="caption">Example of platform scoring (provided by the office of Takae Ito)</p>

For example, an opinion such as "There needs to be discussion about the 1.03 million yen income threshold" would be matched with wage-increase-related policy at a score of 85 points. By contrast, a deeply personal statement such as "I want to die without causing trouble for anyone" was judged to be barely captured by the existing platform.

The combinations between more than 300 policies and 60,000 opinions ran into the hundreds of thousands or even millions. As Yamada put it, it was "an amount that would kill a human being" if done manually, and only became feasible because AI handled the processing. When a single post contained multiple opinions, it was first broken down by intent, and each was then analyzed quantitatively as a separate opinion. The workflow was to list opinions with low match scores, meaning those not covered by the existing platform, and provide that list to the party.

![Figure: Four-step flow from opinion-policy scoring to policy reflection. First, AI scores the match between opinions and platform commitments. Second, it extracts opinions not included in existing commitments. Third, lawmakers discuss the extracted opinions. Fourth, they reflect them in the platform. The “age-18 cliff,” in which support for children with disabilities is cut off after they turn 18, is shown as an example.](images/06_04_scoring_workflow.png)
<p class="caption">Flow from opinion-policy scoring to policy reflection (provided by the office of Takae Ito)</p>

Simply bringing the AI's analysis results into the party would not make them policy. Ito described herself as a "translator," responsible for converting data into the language of politics in conversations with the party executive, the chair of the Policy Research Council, and party lawmakers. "Translation is everything," she said.

### Reflecting the Results in Policy: Discovering the "Age-18 Cliff"

One policy issue brought into sharp relief by the scoring process was the "age-18 cliff." Ito said, "We need to think much more about where children with disabilities can go after they turn 18." There were two problems.

The first was the problem of places to continue learning after graduating from the upper division of special-needs schools. Across Japan, there are only 17 schools where graduates can continue learning, such as advanced courses at special-needs schools: three national, four public, and ten private.

The second was the problem of where to spend the daytime. Children with disabilities can attend after-school day services until they turn 18 and spend time with friends until the evening. But because these are facilities under the Child Welfare Act, they can no longer be used after age 18. As adults, many people then work through continuous employment support services such as Type A and Type B, but work often ends around 3 p.m. As a result, parents providing care are forced to quit their jobs or switch to shorter working hours. Ito said, "Parents raising children with disabilities work hard every day because they want to leave as much as possible for their children after they themselves are gone. Having to quit work despite that is clearly a flaw in the system."

Ito herself is directly affected; she says she became a politician because of her daughter's hearing disability. "I had been told about the age-18 cliff in letters and petitions before," she said. "But not all of those can become party policy. This time, broad listening visualized the voices calling for the age-18 cliff to be resolved as a cluster. It mattered that we obtained quantitative data, not just qualitative data."

As a result, the age-18 cliff became one of the Democratic Party for the People's important policies in the House of Councillors election and even led to the submission of a bill. Other policies were incorporated into the platform through the same process, including "advance care planning," in which people discuss in advance with family and others what medical care and support they would want in an emergency. This was one response to the opinion "I want to die without causing trouble for anyone," which AI scoring had made visible as something the Democratic Party for the People had not adequately addressed.

The draft platform reflecting these AI scoring results was shared with party members and supporters through the Kokumin AI Project before being released to the media. Each item included a Yes/No vote and a free-text field, and 882 people participated. More than 90 percent supported every item, while the free-text comments also included constructive feedback, such as comments that the proposals did not go far enough and suggestions about how far they should go.

### Visualizing Campaign Issues During the Election Period

Broad listening during the House of Councillors campaign captured how voters' concerns shifted in real time. Over the 17-day campaign, the main issues moved dramatically from rising rice prices to 20,000-yen cash payments and tax cuts, and finally to foreign-national issues. In particular, opinions calling for enactment of a bill restricting foreign acquisition of land, which the Democratic Party for the People had independently introduced two years earlier in May 2023, could be seen gaining strength day by day in Talk to the City visualizations.

![Figure: Time-series change in campaign issues. Before the election, the central cluster was policy for food stability through rice-price and distribution reform; early in the campaign, it shifted to livelihood support through income-tax reform and cash payments; from the middle of the campaign onward, it shifted to restrictions on foreign acquisition of land and resources and security-strengthening policy.](images/06_04_topic_shift.png)

Ito said, "The House of Councillors election was a rare election in which the issues changed day by day over the 17-day campaign." In the subsequent House of Representatives election, this kind of issue shift was hardly seen. But in both elections, social media and video platforms clearly had a major effect on the outcome, showing the importance of continuing to track changes in online spaces through broad listening.

### The Challenge of Policy Fact-Checking

The opinion-policy matching system also led to another application of AI. When misinformation spread on social media, the party could use its policies and lawmakers' parliamentary questions from the past five years as evidence and issue a rebuttal as the party's view within two hours[^kokumin3]. This emerged from long-standing concerns: "We want to properly communicate the policy intent behind each piece of misinformation, but we do not have enough people," and "By the time we wait for Community Notes or third-party verification, the election is over."

On the special website for the House of Councillors election, the party published six Q&A-style responses to misinformation[^kokumin10]. At that stage, however, it did not directly quote posts believed to be misinformation, so the effect on stopping their spread was extremely limited.

Even so, the very fact that a political party called its own work "fact-checking" drew criticism. FactCheck Initiative Japan (FIJ) also officially expressed concern that "self-verification by political parties violates international principles and could become a pretext for suppression of speech or censorship"[^kokumin14]. Ito recalled that "it caused quite a firestorm." This experience led to Kokumin Fact in the House of Representatives election.

### A Party Culture That Accepts Market-In Thinking, and the Virtuous Cycle of Broad Listening

For broad listening to function within the party, there was a prerequisite more important than the technology itself. Ito explained it using the marketing contrast between "product-out" and "market-in." A product-out party formulates policy based on its ideals or ideology, then asks the public to support it. A market-in party listens to what the public wants and builds policy from that.

Ito said, "Traditional parties are product-out. But broad listening is something you do for market-in, right?" She added that "there are actually very few parties or politicians who can accept market-in thinking."

As for why a market-in approach was possible in the Democratic Party for the People, Ito cited several factors: it was a party that could hold principles without clinging to ideology; many of its lawmakers came from the private sector, so the argument that "this is a normal method in business" made sense to them; and party leader Tamaki himself actively adopted digital tools and AI. In particular, the large volume of data Tamaki gathered by asking supporters "What do you want me to ask the prime minister?" before speaking in plenary sessions became valuable training data for the Democratic Party for the People's AI.

It was also important that, at the start of the project, Ito secured an initial agreement from the party executive that the AI's analysis results would be reflected in policy. She told senior members, "If the results come out and then, because they differ from my own thinking, we decide to pretend none of it happened, please do not do that. Unless that is clear, I refuse to spend these resources, this money, or my own physical energy." Ito said, "I did not want us to become a party that used AI merely as an image strategy. What implementation required was not only understanding the technology, but also resisting the existing procedures of politics and party decision-making. I recognized that this was the key."

Because Ito performed this translation role, the effort did not end up as broad listening in name only. "Naturally, many opinions came in with the intention of pulling party policy in a particular direction," Ito recalled. "We shared those negative aspects of broad listening within the party as well, and in the end we made decisions after proper discussion among lawmakers."

### The February 2026 House of Representatives Election

The February 2026 House of Representatives election was triggered by a sudden dissolution, so there was nothing like the preparation time available for the House of Councillors election. Ito looked back candidly: "We could hardly create any new mechanisms." Within the limited time available, the main efforts were as follows.

The first was use of Kokumin Club, a platform for supporters. The Democratic Party for the People's traditional information flow was B2B2C: party headquarters to prefectural federations, then local lawmakers, then party members and supporters. Ito said, "Supporters connected to highly active lawmakers would receive information through push-style outreach, but supporters connected to less active lawmakers or overloaded prefectural federations would not receive information. That created differences in experience and dissatisfaction." Kokumin Club is an attempt to communicate through B2C or C2C channels. After a trial operation period that included the campaign, the official version is scheduled to launch this autumn.

The second was the policy fact-checking initiative Kokumin Fact. Despite the criticism during the House of Councillors election, the party decided "this has to be done" and tried again[^kokumin11]. This time, however, unlike the earlier Q&A format, the special site posted screenshots of ordinary users' X posts along with their account names, which became a problem. Criticism poured in, calling it "public shaming of ordinary people" and "encouragement of online mobbing," and there were reports that some of the individuals featured received aggressive direct messages from supporters[^kokumin12]. On February 5, three days before voting day, the issue became a major scandal covered by multiple media outlets. Ito acknowledged "inappropriate operation," deleted all the posts, and apologized[^kokumin13]. The episode once again highlighted the difficulty of political parties conducting fact-checking themselves.

Ito summarized the question of fact-checking by political parties this way: "I am opposed to political parties or politicians conducting general fact-checking. But when it comes to the content of policy, it is also true that only political parties and politicians know the facts, including the intent behind the proposal. An election is an activity in which voters compare each party's policies and cast one vote in an extremely short period of time, so someone needs to present the facts. During election campaigns, the mass media exercise self-restraint. Online media and social media do not have the same constraints. Correctness is not 'justice'; it is accuracy. Because justice differs from person to person, sharing and publishing accurate information, exhausting deliberation, and then following what has been decided are the rules of democracy. During elections, the greatest expression of democracy, the lack of a third party that provides accurate information against false information has become a problem not only in Japan but around the world, creating social division over the question of 'where the truth is.'" The question of who should conduct policy fact-checking remains unresolved.

Ito also argued that, in this House of Representatives election, broad listening was powerless as an election strategy. In a context described as a fandom election, or "Sana-katsu," where enthusiastic support for Prime Minister Takaichi became the reason for voting, policy substance drew relatively little attention. There is also the question of how to reach people who are not interested in elections in the first place, or who do not even have the room to become interested. How can their voiceless voices be heard? The fandom election exposed the limits of the broad listening approach premised on matching policy and opinions.

### The Next Phase of Broad Listening

After two elections, broad listening was facing new challenges. After more than a year of continuously collecting opinions through Talk to the City, the incoming voices had begun to converge, making it harder for genuinely new perspectives to emerge. From the technical support side, Yamada analyzed that "the tendency of the collected data is becoming fixed." Ito also asked, "What do you think the next use case is?"

Two possible directions emerged during the interview.

One is a Q&A system built on the accumulated data. The Democratic Party for the People has more than 300 policies, but even highly committed party members do not necessarily know all of them. As a result, exchanges like "Aren't you doing this policy?" "Actually, we are." "Then isn't this still insufficient?" happen frequently. In the process of building the matching system that connected 60,000 opinions with policy during the House of Councillors election, the party also created a system that can answer such questions immediately. In the future, AI may become a new channel for communication between party members, supporters, and the party.

In fact, there was already a precedent for this direction. In July 2024, an interactive AI called AI Yuichiro, trained on party leader Tamaki's parliamentary questions and social media statements, was released and recorded more than 100,000 uses in 10 days, but it was forced into temporary suspension after only 24 days. OpenAI indicated that use in a political campaign violated its terms, and there were also major cost issues. As Yamada put it, "When usage increases, the cost of AI usage visibly rises."

The other direction is AI-driven deep interviews. Many of the voices gathered through broad listening are surface-level statements such as "I am struggling with X" or "Foreign nationals make me angry," and they do not reveal the underlying causes. If AI could continue the conversation by asking "Why do you feel that way?" or "What triggered that?", it might be able to reach roots of the problem that even the speaker had not fully recognized. During the interview, one proposal was to reconceive this as "online petitioning." If AI could dig into the background of people who come forward with petitions, Ito responded positively, saying it might be possible to "find the missing pieces needed to turn a petition into policy."

How should broad listening go beyond merely collecting opinions, and instead deliver and deepen them? The accumulated practice of broad listening is opening up new possibilities for AI use.

### Seamless Online and Offline Politics, and Broad Listening

Looking back on the two elections, Ito argued that one reason broad listening has become more important is the seamless integration of online and offline campaigning.

"In the 2022 House of Councillors election, I was out on the street as a candidate myself, and I was doing online live streams every day, so I definitely felt that online and offline were connected. But I think the point where they became completely seamless was the 2024 House of Representatives election," Ito said.

As a result of that seamlessness, elections in which social media influence exceeds a party's organizational strength are no longer rare. Ito said that "preparing for elections now requires something completely different from before." It is essential not only for first parties, such as parties and individual politicians, to increase their output on social media and YouTube and heighten engagement through online interaction, but also to strengthen second parties, such as local organizations and supporters who amplify those messages.

"Third parties whose business is video view counts, so to speak, focused attention on Ishimaru in the Tokyo governor election, the Democratic Party for the People in the 2024 House of Representatives election, Sanseito in the 2025 House of Councillors election, and Prime Minister Takaichi in the 2026 House of Representatives election. Their amplification power is tremendous, but unless the first and second parties are already energized, the third parties will not look in their direction," Ito said.

In such a world, companies and political parties alike are expected to understand in real time what is happening on social media and adjust their messaging accordingly. The two experiences of capturing real-time issue shifts in the House of Councillors election and confronting the wall of a fandom election in the House of Representatives race brought both the potential and the limits of broad listening into sharp relief.

### Post-Interview Reflections

What struck me most strongly in this interview was how far the Democratic Party for the People's broad listening had evolved beyond the use in parliamentary questioning introduced in Chapter 4 of this book.

Traditionally, broad listening has been used to grasp mass trends by summarizing and clustering large volumes of opinions, helping campaigns decide what messages to emphasize. This trend-tracking type of broad listening was also used in the House of Councillors election discussed here, but it had evolved beyond taking a snapshot at a single point in time. Instead, it tracked the changing campaign issues in real time over the course of the election.

That said, trend analysis through clustering has a structural limitation. Majority views appear as large visible clusters, but minority voices often fail to form clusters at all and are buried in the summarization process.

What makes the Democratic Party for the People's effort especially noteworthy is that it practiced a form of broad listening that surfaced buried minority voices through full-record matching. Rather than aggregating 60,000 opinions through clustering, it matched every single one individually against more than 300 policies. That meant that even if only one person had raised an issue, if it was not covered by existing policy, it would still emerge as a "low match score opinion." Issues like the age-18 cliff, which had arrived through letters and petitions but had not become party policy, were finally turned into policy through this full-record matching approach.

This went beyond simply identifying trends to win elections; it discovered voices that existing policy had failed to capture. As a case showing that broad listening technology can be used not only to visualize the majority but also to visualize minorities, the Democratic Party for the People's effort deserves special attention.
